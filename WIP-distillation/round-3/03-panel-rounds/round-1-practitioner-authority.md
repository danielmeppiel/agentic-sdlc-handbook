# Practitioner Authority Review — Round 1

## Verdict
**MINOR-REVISIONS**

## Does this help a real developer on Monday morning?

Yes, but with sharp edges. The arc correctly identifies that the current handbook teaches artifacts without teaching the machine—that's the right diagnosis. Ch09 (Runtime Machine) and Ch13 (Deterministic/Probabilistic Boundary) would genuinely help me debug why a primitive that worked in Cursor died in Codex. But Ch17 (Primitives as Code) and Ch18 (Cross-Harness Reference) are 50% teaching prose, 50% reference material disguised as chapters. Ch18 especially reads like a compatibility matrix that belongs in an appendix or docs site, not in the teaching flow. The arc would make Monday easier if we cut or demote Ch18, shorten Ch17 to focus on the transitive-closure question (the only truly novel concept there), and verify Ch09 doesn't bury the practitioner in substrate theory before showing them a single concrete failure it explains.

## Findings

### F1 (severity: high) — Ch18 is a compatibility table, not a chapter
- **Claim:** The proposed Ch18 ("The Cross-Harness Reference") is described as a "5x6 substrate-question matrix" and "structured reference, organized by primitive type, with one row per harness" (02-proposed-arc.md, lines 119–135).
- **Evidence (cite file/section):** 02-proposed-arc.md lines 119–135: "The thesis: The substrate question is not 'does Cursor support skills?' — it is 'in which harness, at which load-lifecycle point, attached to which scope, does this primitive type bind?' The answer is data, not opinion, and the data is concrete and citable." This is reference-material language, not teaching-prose language. The chapter describes a 5x6 matrix with rows per harness — that's a compatibility table.
- **Proposed change:** Demote Ch18 to an appendix or embed it as a living reference on the handbook docs site. If it stays in Part III, shrink it to 800 words maximum, focus on *one* worked example of substrate-question reasoning (e.g., "I wrote a skill that works in Copilot but not in Cursor — here's the matrix row that tells me why"), and point to the full matrix in an appendix. A 2500-word chapter that is primarily tabular data does not earn its place in the practitioner block.

### F2 (severity: medium) — Ch09 risks over-theorizing before showing the payoff
- **Claim:** Ch09 ("The Runtime Machine") is described as teaching "the four-part machine," "markdown-as-code," "the harness is the compiler," and "inference per-thread / filesystem shared" (02-proposed-arc.md, lines 64–79). All four concepts are substrate theory. The chapter structure does not show a concrete failure first.
- **Evidence (cite file/section):** 02-proposed-arc.md lines 64–79: "Key concepts taught: the four-part machine (concept #1)... Markdown-that-steers-an-LLM is code (concept #2)... The harness is the compiler (concept #3)... Inference is per-thread; the filesystem is shared (concept #4)." None of these four bullets starts with "you tried X and it failed because Y." The diagnosis is correct — the handbook doesn't teach the machine — but a 3500-word chapter that opens with a stacked-block diagram of four abstract parts will lose practitioners who need to see the failure mode that the abstraction explains.
- **Proposed change:** Restructure Ch09 to open with a concrete Monday-morning failure: "You wrote a `.github/instructions.md` that worked in Cursor. You switched to Codex and it stopped loading. Here's why." Then introduce the four-part machine as the *explanation* for that failure. Use the abstraction to generalize from the specific, not the other way around. If the opening section (first 500 words) doesn't contain a debugging trace, the chapter is over-theorizing.

### F3 (severity: medium) — Ch17 conflates distribution hygiene with primitive DevEx
- **Claim:** Ch17 ("Primitives as Code") is described as teaching six concepts: transitive-closure question, lint-test-CI, lockfile semantics, bundle leakage, refactor patterns, and portable-vs-vendor-syntax (02-proposed-arc.md, lines 105–112). The first three are about *authoring* primitives; the next two are about *distributing* them; the last is about *design conversations*. That's three different activities in one chapter.
- **Evidence (cite file/section):** 02-proposed-arc.md lines 105–112: "Key concepts taught: The transitive-closure question (concept #7)... Lint-test-CI for primitives (concept #19)... Lockfile semantics for context (concept #20)... Bundle leakage and phantom dependency (concept #22)... Refactor patterns for primitives (concept #27)... The architect stays portable; the coder reaches for vendor syntax (concept #23)." The first three form a coherent "how do I develop and test a primitive?" narrative. The next two (bundle leakage, phantom dep) are *distribution* failures — they only matter when you're shipping a primitive to a registry. The last (portable-vs-vendor) is a design principle. These don't belong in the same chapter.
- **Proposed change:** Split Ch17. Keep the transitive-closure question, lint-test-CI, and lockfile semantics (concepts #7, #19, #20) as the core of Ch17 "Primitives as Code" — this is the Monday-morning question "what closure of files loads when my skill runs?" Move bundle leakage and phantom dep (concepts #22, refactor patterns #27) to Ch16 (Anti-Patterns). Move the portable-vs-vendor discipline (concept #23) to Ch09 as a sidebar. This produces a tighter 1500-word chapter focused on the single most actionable question: "how do I debug what my agent is actually loading?"

### F4 (severity: low) — Ch13 title is too academic
- **Claim:** The proposed title "The Deterministic / Probabilistic Boundary" (02-proposed-arc.md, line 34) sounds like a CS theory paper, not a practitioner handbook.
- **Evidence (cite file/section):** 02-proposed-arc.md line 34: "Ch11.5 (new) **The Deterministic / Probabilistic Boundary**." The thesis (lines 84–86) is clearer: "An agentic system is the composition of two computers — a deterministic one (the harness, file I/O, tool calls) and a probabilistic one (the model)." But the title uses formal language ("deterministic / probabilistic") that a senior dev encountering agentic work for the first time might not immediately map to "the gate that decides what the LLM can mutate."
- **Proposed change:** Retitle to "Two Computers, One Program: Where the Gate Sits" or "The Seam Between the Model and the Harness" — use plain language. The current title is precise but not inviting. A practitioner flipping to this chapter needs to immediately recognize "oh, this is about where I put the guard rails."

### F5 (severity: high) — Concrete-example inventory is missing from the arc document
- **Claim:** The proposed arc (02-proposed-arc.md) describes four new chapters but does not list their concrete Monday-morning examples in the chapter descriptions. For a practitioner-block arc, this is a structural gap.
- **Evidence (cite file/section):** 02-proposed-arc.md sections 4 (new chapters in detail, lines 63–137) describe Ch09, Ch13, Ch17, Ch18. Each section has a "Key concepts taught" bullet list and a "Diagram inventory." None have a "Worked examples" or "Concrete scenarios" section. Ch09 mentions "a worked example: a 30-line `.instructions.md` file" (line 71) but does not describe the scenario. Ch13 mentions "Worked example: a gh-aw workflow with `safe-outputs:`" (line 89) but does not name the failure it prevents. Ch17 mentions "Worked example from the APM publish-time rules" (line 110) but does not specify the actual bundle-leakage case. Ch18 has no worked examples listed.
- **Proposed change:** Before implementation, write a one-paragraph "Concrete scenarios" section for each new chapter. Each paragraph must describe: (1) the Monday-morning task the practitioner is trying to do, (2) the failure they hit, (3) the concept from the chapter that explains the failure, (4) the fix. This is the minimal standard for practitioner content. Without this, we risk shipping substrate theory that reads well but doesn't land when a developer is staring at a broken skill.

### F6 (severity: medium) — The arc does not surface the "what breaks without it" test
- **Claim:** The persona file (handbook-practitioner-authority.agent.md, line 26) states: "Every technique includes: what it is, when to use it, how to implement it, what breaks without it." The proposed arc does not explicitly verify that each new chapter teaches the failure mode.
- **Evidence (cite file/section):** handbook-practitioner-authority.agent.md line 26: "Every technique includes: what it is, when to use it, how to implement it, what breaks without it." The arc document (02-proposed-arc.md) describes "Key concepts taught" and "Diagram inventory" but does not have a parallel "Failure modes" or "What breaks without this" section. Ch13 implies it (the entire chapter is about the failure of putting side effects on the wrong side of the seam), but Ch09, Ch17, and Ch18 do not explicitly name their anti-patterns.
- **Proposed change:** Add a "Failure modes" section to each new chapter's detail block in the arc. For Ch09: "what breaks when you don't understand the runtime machine? (You write a primitive that works in one harness but silently fails in another because the loader is different.)" For Ch17: "what breaks when you don't track transitive closure? (Your skill works on your machine because you have implicit context files loaded, but fails for a teammate who doesn't.)" For Ch18: if it stays, "what breaks when you don't consult the matrix? (You design a multi-agent topology that assumes programmatic spawn, then discover your chosen harness doesn't support it.)" This is the "show the failure mode" principle applied at arc-planning time.

### F7 (severity: low) — The "seven outcome competencies" are listed but not testable
- **Claim:** The directionality contract (02-proposed-arc.md, lines 11–20) lists seven competencies the reader should have after finishing the practitioner block. These are the right competencies, but they are not written in testable form.
- **Evidence (cite file/section):** 02-proposed-arc.md lines 11–20: "1. Name the four parts of the runtime machine... 2. Reason about how their primitives load... 3. Recognize attention as a physical property... 4. Locate the deterministic / probabilistic seam... 5. Diagnose silent semantic failures... 6. Treat their primitives as code... 7. Translate a design from one harness to another..." These are outcomes, but they're not testable. "Name the four parts" is testable. "Reason about how primitives load" is not — what does passing look like?
- **Proposed change:** Rewrite the seven competencies in testable form before implementation. Examples: "2. Given a primitive with three external dependencies, draw the transitive-closure tree and identify which files load in which order." "4. Given a gh-aw workflow, mark which steps are deterministic and which are probabilistic." "5. Given a skill that worked in dev but failed in CI, use the substrate-question matrix to diagnose which harness property caused the divergence." Testable outcomes force precision. The current list is directionally correct but too abstract to validate against the delivered prose.

## Concrete-example check

Based on the arc document (02-proposed-arc.md, section 4), here are the concrete examples listed for each new chapter:

- **Ch09 (Runtime Machine):** One example: "a 30-line `.instructions.md` file is parsed, linked, loaded, and executed" (line 71). One comparison: "an APM project running under Copilot vs the same project running under Claude Code" (line 70). **Status: present but thin.** The example needs to show a *failure* (e.g., the file works in Copilot but not Claude Code) and the diagnosis, not just the mechanics.

- **Ch13 (Deterministic/Probabilistic Boundary):** One example: "a gh-aw workflow with `safe-outputs:` translating model intent into bounded, auditable mutations" (line 89). **Status: present.** This is a strong example if it shows the failure mode (what happens when you don't use `safe-outputs:`).

- **Ch17 (Primitives as Code):** One example: "Worked example from the APM publish-time rules" (line 110). **Status: vague.** This needs to be a specific case: "I published a skill that included my `.github/workflows/` directory, and now every consumer's CI runs my maintainer scripts — here's how to fix it."

- **Ch18 (Cross-Harness Reference):** Zero examples listed. **Status: absent.** This is a finding. If Ch18 stays as a chapter (rather than demoted to an appendix), it needs at least one worked scenario: "I wrote a multi-agent workflow that assumes programmatic spawn. I look up 'spawn' in the matrix and discover Cursor doesn't support it. Here's how I restructure to use file-based coordination instead."

**Flagged as findings:** F5 (missing concrete-example inventory in the arc document) and the Ch18 absence noted here.

## Final word

This arc is structurally sound and correctly diagnoses the gap: the current handbook teaches artifacts without teaching the machine. The four new chapters (Ch09, Ch13, Ch17, Ch18) would make Monday morning easier *if* they are written with concrete failures first and substrate theory second. My biggest concern is that Ch18 is a compatibility table masquerading as teaching prose — demote it to an appendix or embed it as a living reference. My second concern is that Ch09 risks opening with 1000 words of four-part-machine abstraction before showing the practitioner why they need to care. Start with the failure, then explain the machine. Ch17 needs to be split or tightened — the transitive-closure question is the Monday-morning win, but it's buried under five other concepts. If the authors fix F1 (demote Ch18), F2 (reorder Ch09 to failure-first), F3 (split Ch17), and F5 (write the concrete-example inventory before implementation), this arc ships. The seven outcome competencies are the right ones; the concepts are the right ones; the execution plan just needs to prove it won't over-theorize.
